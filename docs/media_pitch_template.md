# VERDICT Media Pitch Templates

Operational rulebook for all journalist and media outreach sent from the VERDICT operator. All pitches generated by the Operations Project for media must follow one of the templates in this document.

This document works in combination with:

- `qa_protocol_v1-1.md` — 3-pass review applied to every media pitch before sending
- `confirmed_strategic_decisions.md` — Media and distribution policy section defines binding constraints
- `vendor_notification_template.md` — when a pitch references a specific vendor, the vendor engagement rules still apply
- `linkedin_posting_rules.md` — when a pitch accompanies a LinkedIn post, both rule sets apply

If any rule here appears to conflict with `confirmed_strategic_decisions.md` (Media and distribution section), the confirmed decisions win.

---

## Core principles

1. **Source offer, not coverage request.** Every pitch positions VERDICT as providing data the journalist can verify and use. Never ask for coverage, features, mentions, or quotes. The value proposition is the data itself.
2. **Public data only.** All evidence cited in pitches comes from sources the journalist can independently verify: NVD, CISA KEV, GitHub Security Advisories, official ToS, published vendor documentation. No private briefings, no embargoed vendor statements, no unpublished analysis.
3. **Proactive bias disclosure.** Every pitch discloses upfront that VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic tooling), and that Anthropic operates in the AI agent market evaluated by VERDICT. This disclosure appears near the opening, not buried in a footer.
4. **No exclusivity offers.** Identical pitches may be sent to multiple journalists. Exclusivity offers compromise editorial independence by creating an implicit obligation to the exclusive recipient. If a journalist asks for exclusivity, decline politely and explain the position.
5. **Citation expectation.** When a journalist cites VERDICT data in published coverage, the expected citation format includes a link to the VERDICT framework documentation (https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine) in addition to any data-specific URLs. This is stated in the pitch so journalists understand the citation norm before drafting.
6. **No pre-publication coordination.** Pitches never offer to review a journalist's draft before publication. Pitches never request that VERDICT be quoted in a particular way. The journalist retains full editorial control; VERDICT provides source material only.

## Template 1: Cold pitch to journalist

Use this template for the initial outreach to a journalist who has not previously corresponded with VERDICT. This is the most common media pitch scenario during Phase 1 distribution.

A cold pitch introduces VERDICT, offers a specific story hook with cited data, and leaves the journalist with everything they need to verify independently. The pitch does not request coverage; it offers source material. Whether the journalist uses the material is their editorial decision.

### When this template applies

All three conditions must be met:

- The journalist has not previously replied to any VERDICT communication
- A specific story hook is identifiable (recent incident, category analysis, notable platform evaluation, or framework finding)
- The journalist's beat or recent coverage includes AI industry, enterprise security, developer tooling, or adjacent topics where VERDICT data is relevant

Do not use this template for:

- General awareness outreach without a specific story hook (use LinkedIn posts instead; see `linkedin_posting_rules.md`)
- Vendor-adjacent journalists (PR contacts, industry analysts who work for vendor-funded research firms) — these require different channels
- Certain journalist-suitability edge cases are routed for internal review before pitching.

### Journalist identification

Before pitching, document the following in the media outreach tracker:

- `{{JOURNALIST_NAME}}` — full name as used in bylines
- `{{JOURNALIST_BEAT}}` — their stated beat (from publication bio or recent article patterns)
- `{{PUBLICATION}}` — publication name (use the form journalists use: `The Register`, not `theregister.com`)
- `{{PUBLICATION_SECTION}}` — specific section if relevant (`Security`, `AI/ML`, `Enterprise`)
- `{{JOURNALIST_EMAIL}}` — verified contact email, typically `firstname.lastname@publication` format
- `{{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE}}` — at least one recent article by the journalist that signals fit with the pitch topic
- `{{SEARCH_DATE}}` — date the journalist was identified, for tracking outreach freshness

The `{{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE}}` field is structural, not decorative. Referencing the journalist's prior work in the pitch demonstrates that VERDICT has read their coverage and is not sending a generic blast. This materially improves open and response rates.

Source the journalist's email address through one of:

- Publication's staff page or author bio (most authoritative)
- Recent article footer or author signature
- Journalist's personal website or LinkedIn profile
- Standard corporate email format inference (`firstname.lastname@publication.com`) — use as last resort, verify by cross-reference

Never use email-guessing tools, scraped databases, or aggregator services whose data provenance is unclear. If verified contact is not available, do not send the pitch.

### Subject line

Use one of the following patterns. Select the variant that most precisely describes the story hook:

- Incident-driven: `{{INCIDENT_OR_EVENT}} — independent data from VERDICT AI agent trust index`
- Category analysis: `AI agent trust scores across {{CATEGORY}} — VERDICT dataset, {{NUMBER}} platforms evaluated`
- Notable platform finding: `{{PLATFORM_NAME}} VERDICT evaluation — {{KEY_DATA_POINT}}`
- Framework reference: `Independent AI agent trust framework — methodology and {{NUMBER}}-platform dataset`

Subject line rules:

- Must be ≤ 80 characters (journalists' email clients truncate longer lines)
- Must not use sensational language (no "BREAKING," no "EXCLUSIVE," no "URGENT")
- Must not ask a question
- Must include at least one specific factual anchor (a number, a name, or an event date)
- Must contain the word "VERDICT" so the journalist can identify and route pitches in the future

### Opening paragraph

Draft the opening in the following structure:

```
Dear {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

I'm writing from VERDICT, an independent AI agent trust evaluation index. I noticed your recent {{PUBLICATION_SECTION}} coverage, including {{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE_TITLE}} ({{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE_URL}}), and thought VERDICT's dataset may be relevant to a story you are considering or may consider in the future.

VERDICT is operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan) and publishes evaluations using only public data sources. A bias disclosure note: VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), and Anthropic is an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates. This disclosure is included in every VERDICT evaluation and in all media communications.
```

The opening is fixed in structure. Do not add flattery, general industry commentary, or self-promotion. The three sentences carry exactly three functions: identification, relevance signal to the journalist's beat, and bias disclosure.

The relevance signal (citing the journalist's recent coverage) is structural, not decorative. It demonstrates that this pitch is targeted rather than a blast, which materially affects how the pitch is received and whether it passes the journalist's internal spam filter.

Rules for the opening:

- Always name a specific prior article by the journalist. If no relevant recent article exists, do not pitch — the journalist is not a fit for the story.
- The bias disclosure sentence is verbatim. Do not abbreviate as "bias note" or "disclosure:" — the full wording signals institutional rigor.
- Do not open with "I hope this finds you well" or similar filler. Journalists read hundreds of pitches; filler openings signal inattention to their time.
- Do not describe VERDICT as "leading," "pioneering," "the first," or any superlative. These claims cannot be substantiated at Phase 1 scale and damage credibility.

### Story hook

The story hook is the specific reason this pitch is worth the journalist's attention. It must be concrete, data-anchored, and independently verifiable. Include a hook paragraph immediately after the opening:

```
The specific hook: {{STORY_HOOK}}

Supporting data from VERDICT's evaluation of {{PLATFORM_NAME}} (published {{EVALUATION_DATE}} at {{VERDICT_EVALUATION_URL}}):

- {{KEY_DATA_POINT_1}}
- {{KEY_DATA_POINT_2}}
- {{KEY_DATA_POINT_3}}

Relevant context for your beat:

{{CONTEXT_PARAGRAPH}}

All data cites public sources listed on the evaluation page.
```

Rules for the story hook:

- The hook must be one sentence. If the hook cannot be expressed in one sentence, the pitch lacks focus and should be restructured.
- The three key data points are ordered by relevance to the journalist's beat, not by VERDICT's internal priority. A security journalist sees R and T dimension data first; a business journalist sees score and tier data first.
- The context paragraph is 2–3 sentences maximum. Any longer reads as argumentation rather than source provision.
- Never include more than three key data points. Journalists receive abridged information well; they reject long bullet lists.
- Never include VERDICT's own interpretation or commentary beyond what is already published on the evaluation page. The journalist may form their own interpretation from the cited data.

### Framework reference

After the story hook, include a framework reference block so the journalist can verify the methodology independently:

```
About the VERDICT framework:

- Methodology: {{NUMBER}}-point evaluation across seven dimensions (Verifiability, Effectiveness, Resilience, Data Conduct, Identity & Control, Containment, Transparency)
- Data sources: public only (NVD, CISA KEV, GitHub Security Advisories, official ToS, vendor documentation)
- Framework documentation: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine
- Current dataset: {{NUMBER}} platforms evaluated, updated continuously
- Independence: zero vendor funding, no paid certifications, no pre-publication vendor sharing

Citation norm: when VERDICT data appears in published coverage, please link both the specific evaluation page and the framework documentation so readers can verify the methodology alongside the finding.
```

Rules for the framework reference:

- The framework URL is always the repository root, not a specific file. Journalists verify by browsing; a root URL gives them entry to README, ENGINE.md, and QA.md in sequence.
- Never hide the framework URL in a footer. Its presence in the pitch body signals that the data is open to review, which differentiates VERDICT from vendor-funded research.
- The citation norm sentence is not optional. It establishes, in writing and pre-publication, the expectation that both URLs travel together in citations. This is the single most important line for long-term institutional recognition of VERDICT.
- Do not describe the framework as "proprietary," "patent-pending," or with any scarcity language. The framework is CC-BY 4.0. Journalists interpret scarcity language as vendor-style positioning.

### Self-serve verification

After the framework reference, explicitly invite the journalist to verify independently before any conversation with VERDICT:

```
If helpful for your reporting, you can verify the data in this pitch independently before any conversation with VERDICT:

- Evaluation page with full dimension-level detail: {{VERDICT_EVALUATION_URL}}
- Public sources cited in the evaluation (direct links on the evaluation page)
- Framework methodology and scoring rubric: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine/blob/main/ENGINE.md
- Known Fact Corrections (factual entries VERDICT has cross-referenced against vendor claims): https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine/blob/main/KNOWN_FACTS.md

VERDICT is happy to answer questions, but the data supports independent reporting without any input from VERDICT.
```

Rules for the self-serve verification block:

- The statement "the data supports independent reporting without any input from VERDICT" is structural. It removes the implicit pressure that engaging with VERDICT is a precondition for using the data. Journalists under deadline appreciate this framing.
- Always link to ENGINE.md and KNOWN_FACTS.md directly (not just the repository root). For methodology questions, ENGINE.md is the entry point; for fact-verification questions, KNOWN_FACTS.md is the authoritative record.
- Do not offer expert commentary or briefing calls in this section. If the journalist wants those, they will request them. Offering them upfront signals that VERDICT is seeking coverage, which contradicts the source-offer posture.
- The order of verification resources is: specific evaluation page → public sources → framework methodology → known facts. This mirrors how a journalist verifies a data point: specific claim first, then supporting evidence, then methodology context, then edge cases.

### Closing

Close the pitch with the following block:

```
VERDICT operates on a 90-day routine re-evaluation cycle, with earlier re-evaluations triggered by CVE disclosures (CVSS 7.0+), CISA KEV listings, supply chain compromises affecting evaluated platforms, or significant changes published in vendor documentation. Future coverage based on this dataset remains current through these triggers.

For follow-up questions or additional data requests: press@getverdict.fyi
For factual corrections or clarification on specific claims: vendor@getverdict.fyi

VERDICT is an independent AI agent trust evaluation index, operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan). All evaluations use public data exclusively. No vendor funds, sponsors, or receives pre-publication access to evaluations.

— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)
```

Rules for the Template 1 closing:

- The re-evaluation cycle reference is structural: it tells the journalist that a story published today based on VERDICT data remains anchored to a live, maintained dataset rather than a one-off report.
- The identity statement block is repeated (same reasoning as vendor notification Template 1: forwarding resilience). A pitch forwarded to an editor or a fact-checker must carry the full context on its own.
- Two email addresses are listed with explicit routing logic: `press@` for journalistic follow-up, `vendor@` for factual correction requests. This mirrors vendor notification routing and keeps the correspondence model consistent across VERDICT's external communications.
- The signature is `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)` — the `(via press@...)` parenthetical clarifies the sender alias, which supports journalists who organize inbound pitches by source.
- Do not add postscripts, urgency prompts, or suggested publication timelines. The closing block is the final element.

### Template 1 quality check

Before sending a cold pitch, verify each of the following. If any item fails, revise the pitch or handle per the internal escalation procedure.

Content checks:

- The story hook is one sentence and names a specific, dated event or finding
- The three key data points reference specific numbers or named entities (not categories or generalities)
- The recent relevant coverage citation names a specific prior article by the journalist (not "your recent work")
- Every factual claim traces to a public URL either in the pitch or on the linked evaluation page
- The bias disclosure sentence appears verbatim in the opening
- The citation norm sentence appears in the framework reference section
- No superlative language describing VERDICT ("leading," "first," "pioneering")
- No expert commentary offered upfront (no briefing calls, no private analysis)
- No pressure language ("urgent," "time-sensitive," "embargoed" unless using Template 4)

Format checks:

- Total pitch length: 350–500 words (journalists expect this range; shorter signals under-preparation, longer signals low respect for time)
- Subject line ≤ 80 characters
- Three key data points only (not four, not two)
- Email body paragraphs separated by blank lines (not by section headers with `###` or similar, which misreads as Markdown artifact in email clients)

Relationship checks:

- The journalist has not received a pitch from VERDICT in the last 14 days (prevent pitch fatigue)
- The journalist's publication is not currently the subject of a VERDICT evaluation concern (conflict-of-interest surface)
- The journalist has not publicly stated they do not accept pitches (some journalists list this on their bio)

- Variant creation is governed by the internal review procedure (do not introduce new variants outside it; the "source offer, not coverage request" posture must be preserved).

## Template 2: Follow-up pitch (no prior response)

Use this template when a Cold pitch (Template 1) has received no response and the underlying story hook remains relevant. The follow-up is not a re-pitch of the same content; it introduces a new angle, a new data point, or a new relevance signal that justifies a second outreach.

Follow-ups without new content are pitch fatigue. Journalists remember VERDICT negatively if they receive two pitches on the same story within a short window. This template exists to prevent that failure mode by enforcing a new-angle requirement.

### When this template applies

All four conditions must be met:

- The original Cold pitch (Template 1) was sent and received no reply within 14 days
- A specific new angle, data point, or relevance signal has emerged since the original pitch
- The journalist's beat and recent coverage still indicate fit (verify — beats shift)
- No other VERDICT pitch has been sent to this journalist in the interim

Do not use this template for:

- Nudging the journalist to respond to the original pitch (this reads as pressure, not source offer)
- Repeating the original pitch with slightly different wording (journalists recognize this pattern immediately)
- Following up more than once on the same story hook (after two attempts with no response, the journalist is not the fit — remove from the outreach list for this story and return to the database for future stories only)

### Timing

Send the follow-up 14–21 days after the original Cold pitch:

- **Under 14 days**: reads as impatient, signals that VERDICT is tracking response times anxiously
- **Over 21 days**: the original pitch context has decayed from the journalist's memory; the follow-up reads as a fresh cold pitch without the "new angle" framing working as intended
- **14–21 days is the window** where the journalist recalls the original pitch context and the new angle lands as a genuine update

Exceptions:

- If the new angle is directly tied to a breaking event (incident, acquisition, CVE disclosure), send the follow-up within 48 hours of the event regardless of the 14-day minimum. Time-sensitivity that the journalist can independently verify changes the pitch economics.
- If more than 30 days have passed, do not use Template 2 — treat the journalist as a new Cold pitch target (Template 1) and construct a fresh pitch without referencing the prior outreach.

### Subject line

Use one of the following patterns. The subject line must explicitly signal a follow-up so the journalist can immediately identify the context:

- New angle: `VERDICT follow-up: {{PLATFORM_NAME_OR_TOPIC}} — {{NEW_ANGLE_SUMMARY}}`
- New data: `VERDICT follow-up: {{PLATFORM_NAME_OR_TOPIC}} — updated data point {{KEY_DATA_POINT}}`
- Incident-linked: `VERDICT follow-up: {{PLATFORM_NAME_OR_TOPIC}} — {{INCIDENT_NAME}} update`

Subject line rules:

- The word "follow-up" must appear explicitly. Concealing the follow-up nature damages trust when the journalist recognizes the prior pitch.
- Do not use "Re:" prefix artificially (by copying the original subject line into a new message). Either reply within the original thread or start fresh — pretending to thread when no reply exists misleads the journalist's inbox filtering.
- Do not use "Checking in," "Circling back," "Touching base," or any relationship-maintenance phrases. These signal that the follow-up is about the relationship rather than new content.
- Character limit and anchor rules from Template 1 apply (≤ 80 chars, no sensationalism, no questions, at least one specific anchor).

### Opening paragraph (Follow-up)

The follow-up opening differs from the Cold pitch opening in three ways: it explicitly references the prior outreach, it names the new angle upfront, and it omits the journalist-prior-coverage citation (already established in the original pitch). Draft the opening in the following structure:

```
Dear {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

Following up on my earlier note about {{ORIGINAL_PITCH_TOPIC}} (sent {{ORIGINAL_PITCH_DATE}}). A new angle has emerged that may be relevant: {{NEW_ANGLE_SUMMARY}}.

VERDICT is operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan) and publishes evaluations using only public data sources. A bias disclosure note: VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), and Anthropic is an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates. This disclosure is included in every VERDICT evaluation and in all media communications.
```

The follow-up opening is fixed in structure. The three sentences carry exactly three functions: reference to prior pitch, new angle disclosure, and bias disclosure (repeated for forwarding resilience and for journalists who read pitches in isolation from their history).

Rules for the follow-up opening:

- Reference the original pitch by topic, not by subject line. Journalists remember topics; they rarely remember subject lines.
- The original pitch date is included so the journalist can locate the prior email if desired. This is a time-respect signal.
- The new angle is stated in the opening, not held for the body. If the new angle needs the body to explain, it is not strong enough to justify a follow-up.
- The bias disclosure is repeated verbatim. The forwarding resilience rationale from vendor notification Template 1 applies here: follow-up emails are sometimes read in isolation from the original pitch by the journalist's editor or fact-checker.
- Do not apologize for the follow-up ("sorry to bother again," "apologies if this is a repeat"). Apologies signal that the outreach is intrusive, which contradicts the source-offer posture.

### New angle body

The body of the follow-up presents the new angle with supporting data, in the same structural pattern as Template 1's story hook but with tighter constraints given that the journalist has already seen the surrounding context:

```
What's new since the original pitch:

{{NEW_ANGLE_DESCRIPTION}}

Supporting data:

- {{NEW_DATA_POINT_1}}
- {{NEW_DATA_POINT_2}}

How this connects to the original story hook:

{{CONNECTION_PARAGRAPH}}

Evaluation page with full context: {{VERDICT_EVALUATION_URL}}
```

Rules for the new angle body:

- Only two new data points (not three). Follow-ups warrant a tighter information budget because the journalist has prior context. Over-loading with data signals that the new angle is weak and is being padded.
- The connection paragraph (2 sentences maximum) explicitly ties the new angle to the original story hook. This is not decorative — without the explicit connection, the follow-up reads as a fresh cold pitch rather than a genuine update.
- Do not restate the framework reference block from Template 1. The journalist already received it in the original pitch. Restating it signals inattention to their time.
- Do not restate the self-serve verification block either. Link only to the specific evaluation page; the journalist has the framework URL from the original pitch.
- Never ask whether the journalist received the original pitch. This shifts focus to the relationship status rather than the new data.

### Closing (Follow-up)

Close the follow-up with a compressed version of Template 1's closing. The journalist has the framework and routing context from the original pitch; the follow-up closing reinforces only the essentials:

```
Evaluation page: {{VERDICT_EVALUATION_URL}}

For factual corrections or follow-up questions: press@getverdict.fyi

VERDICT is an independent AI agent trust evaluation index, operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan). All evaluations use public data exclusively. No vendor funds, sponsors, or receives pre-publication access to evaluations.

— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)
```

Rules for the Template 2 closing:

- The compressed closing (four short blocks versus Template 1's seven lines) respects the journalist's time and signals that the follow-up is supplementary rather than a full re-introduction.
- The identity statement is repeated, not removed. Forwarding resilience applies here too — a follow-up forwarded to an editor may be read without the original pitch attached.
- Only one email address is listed (`press@`). Factual-correction routing is not relevant in a follow-up context; simplicity serves the journalist's inbox triage.
- Do not include a re-evaluation cycle reference. The journalist already has this from the original pitch.
- The signature remains `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)`.

### Template 2 quality check

Before sending a follow-up pitch, verify each of the following. If any item fails, do not send — either wait for a stronger new angle or remove the journalist from this story's outreach list.

Content checks:

- The new angle is substantive, not a rephrasing of the original pitch
- The new angle is stated in one sentence in the opening
- Exactly two new data points are included (not one, not three)
- The connection paragraph ties the new angle to the original story hook explicitly
- No apologetic or relationship-maintenance language appears anywhere

Timing checks:

- At least 14 days have passed since the original Cold pitch (or 48 hours if tied to a breaking event, or the event is ongoing)
- Fewer than 30 days have passed since the original pitch (otherwise revert to Template 1 for a fresh pitch)
- No other VERDICT pitch has been sent to this journalist in the interim

Relationship checks:

- This is the first follow-up on this specific story hook (not a second or third)
- The journalist's beat remains a fit for the pitch topic
- The journalist has not signaled disinterest in VERDICT data (through a prior negative reply or public statement)

## Template 3: Warm pitch to returning journalist

Use this template when pitching a journalist who has previously engaged with VERDICT — by replying to a prior pitch, citing VERDICT in published coverage, or otherwise signaling active interest. The warm pitch leverages the existing relationship context without overstepping into presumed familiarity.

A warm pitch is not a casual pitch. The increased intimacy of the journalist relationship amplifies both the opportunity (higher response rate, established trust) and the risk (a misjudged pitch damages a relationship that took effort to build). This template enforces discipline appropriate to that asymmetric stake.

### When this template applies

At least one of the following must be true, and none of the exclusions apply:

**Qualifying history (at least one required):**

- The journalist previously replied to a VERDICT pitch (Template 1, 2, 4, or 5) with any substantive content, including decline-with-reasoning
- The journalist has cited VERDICT in published coverage within the last 6 months
- The journalist has requested specific VERDICT data via Template 6 (Response to media inquiry) within the last 12 months
- The journalist has attended a VERDICT-adjacent event or publicly referenced VERDICT in their own writing (e.g., social posts)

**Exclusions (any of these disqualifies a warm pitch):**

- The journalist's most recent interaction was a firm decline ("please stop pitching me," "this is not relevant to my beat")
- More than 12 months have elapsed since the last interaction (the relationship has cooled; treat as Cold pitch via Template 1 with a recognition phrase)
- The journalist's publication has changed in a way that shifts their coverage scope away from VERDICT-relevant topics
- Certain prior-coverage conflict cases are handled per the internal escalation procedure before any pitch

### Relationship history documentation

Before sending a warm pitch, document the following in the media outreach tracker:

- `{{PRIOR_INTERACTION_TYPE}}` — one of: `replied to pitch`, `cited in coverage`, `inquired via Template 6`, `referenced in own writing`
- `{{PRIOR_INTERACTION_DATE}}` — date of the most recent qualifying interaction
- `{{PRIOR_COVERAGE_TITLE}}` — if applicable: title of the article that cited VERDICT
- `{{PRIOR_COVERAGE_URL}}` — if applicable: URL of that article
- `{{PRIOR_CONVERSATION_SUBSTANCE}}` — brief description of what was discussed or what angle the journalist previously pursued (one sentence, factual)
- `{{RELATIONSHIP_CADENCE}}` — how frequently prior pitches have been sent to this journalist (count in last 12 months)

The `{{RELATIONSHIP_CADENCE}}` field enforces pitch-frequency discipline. A journalist who has received more than 4 pitches in the last 12 months is approaching fatigue territory even with prior positive engagement. If the cadence is already high, reconsider whether the current pitch is strong enough to justify another outreach; if in doubt, defer.

### Subject line

Use one of the following patterns. Warm pitch subject lines reference the prior context more directly than cold pitches:

- Direct reference: `VERDICT data relevant to {{PRIOR_COVERAGE_TOPIC}} — {{NEW_ANGLE_SUMMARY}}`
- Continuation: `Follow-on data for the {{PRIOR_COVERAGE_TOPIC}} angle — VERDICT evaluation update`
- New story, prior journalist: `New VERDICT finding for {{JOURNALIST_BEAT}} — {{KEY_DATA_POINT}}`

Subject line rules:

- Subject lines may reference prior coverage by topic, but never by specific article title. Article titles lock the pitch into the prior framing; topics allow the new angle to stand independently.
- The word "VERDICT" still appears explicitly, even though the journalist recognizes the source. Consistency across cold, follow-up, and warm pitches supports inbox searchability.
- Do not use subject lines that read as thread continuations ("Re: our last conversation," "Following up on our chat"). These subject lines are reserved for actual reply threading; using them without an active thread misleads the journalist's email client.
- Character limit and anchor rules from Template 1 apply (≤ 80 chars, no sensationalism, no questions, at least one specific anchor).

### Opening paragraph (Warm pitch)

The warm pitch opening differs from the Cold pitch opening in three ways: it references the prior relationship context by specific type (replied pitch / citation / inquiry / public reference), it omits the full VERDICT identification sentence (already established), and it moves directly into the new angle. Draft the opening using the variant that matches the relationship history type.

**Variant W-1: Prior reply relationship**

Use when the journalist previously replied to a VERDICT pitch with substantive content.

```
Dear {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

Thank you again for your {{PRIOR_INTERACTION_DATE}} note on the {{PRIOR_PITCH_TOPIC}} pitch. A new finding in VERDICT's dataset may be relevant to your continued {{JOURNALIST_BEAT}} coverage: {{NEW_ANGLE_SUMMARY}}.

A bias disclosure note (for the record, and in case this pitch is forwarded): VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), and Anthropic is an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates.
```

Rules for warm pitch openings:

- Select exactly one variant. Do not hybridize variants even if multiple relationship types apply — pick the strongest and most recent.

- Never reference past rejections positively ("thanks for your honest decline"). This reads as ironic or passive-aggressive regardless of intent. Either the decline qualifies the journalist for future pitches or it does not; no acknowledgement of the decline itself is needed.
- The new angle appears in the first paragraph. Warm pitches compress the ramp-up that Cold pitches require.

Rules for the warm pitch body:

- Three data points, same count as Cold pitch. The journalist retains a dataset mental model and can absorb the full data at this point.
- The continuity paragraph (2–3 sentences) explicitly connects the new finding to the journalist's prior coverage, specific question, or expressed interest. This is stronger than Template 1's context paragraph because the warm relationship permits specific references.
- Do not restate the full framework reference block. Link only to the specific evaluation page in the body and to the framework root in the closing (below).
- Do not ask whether the journalist would be interested. The data is offered; interest is the journalist's decision.
- Never imply ownership of the coverage angle. Phrases like "our story" or "the story we built together" overclaim the relationship — the journalist owns the coverage, always.

Rules for the Template 3 closing:

- The routing lines (press@ and vendor@) remain, since warm relationships sometimes surface corrections that the journalist would prefer to route formally rather than as a casual reply.
- The "no acknowledgement needed" paragraph is unique to Template 3. Explicitly removing that pressure strengthens the relationship's durability.
- The "VERDICT does not track open-rates or response-rates" line is factually accurate and distinguishes VERDICT from PR-agency pitches that track journalist engagement. This factual distinction is institutional in posture.
- The identity statement is omitted in Template 3 closing. The journalist already knows VERDICT; restating the identity in a warm pitch reads as inattention to the relationship context.
- The signature remains `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)`.

- No ownership-implying language ("our story," "the coverage we built")

Tone checks:

## Template 4: Incident-driven scoop offer

Use this template when a newly disclosed incident (CVE, CISA KEV listing, supply chain compromise, major vendor event) intersects with one or more VERDICT evaluations, and time-sensitive data from the dataset can materially inform coverage of the incident. The scoop offer positions VERDICT as an independently verifiable source for incident coverage, not as a source of new investigation.

Incident-driven pitches operate on a tighter time budget than Cold pitches. Journalists covering an incident have hours, not days, to integrate external sources. This template enforces brevity, specificity, and immediate verifiability. The pitch is sent to multiple journalists simultaneously — exclusivity is never offered (Core principle 4).

### When this template applies

All four conditions must be met:

- A specific incident has been publicly disclosed within the last 72 hours (CVE publication date, CISA KEV listing date, vendor security advisory publication, or first-party press release)
- VERDICT has at least one published evaluation for a platform materially affected by or structurally adjacent to the incident
- The VERDICT data offers a perspective a journalist cannot quickly derive from the incident disclosure itself (cross-platform context, pre-incident evaluation history, or dimension-level analysis)
- The incident is substantive enough that responsible journalists will cover it (CVSS 7.0+, CISA KEV addition, or independent multi-source confirmation)

Do not use this template for:

- Incidents more than 72 hours old (coverage cycle has peaked; treat as retrospective analysis under Template 1 or 5 instead)
- Minor CVEs (CVSS < 7.0) without KEV listing or independent coverage signal
- Rumored or unconfirmed incidents (VERDICT does not amplify speculation)
- Incidents involving VERDICT itself or its operator: no outbound pitch (handled per the internal escalation procedure)

### Coordination considerations

Unlike Cold or Warm pitches, the incident-driven scoop offer is sent to **multiple journalists simultaneously** as a standard practice. Several operational considerations follow:

- The journalist selection list is curated before the pitch is drafted. Typical list: 4–8 journalists whose beats cover the incident type (security journalists for CVE-driven incidents, AI industry journalists for AI-specific events, investigative journalists for supply chain events).
- The pitch text is identical across all recipients. No customization beyond the salutation is permitted, because customization creates divergent factual claims across a live news cycle — a failure mode that damages institutional credibility if any journalist notices.
- All pitches go out within a 30-minute window. Staggered sending creates perceptions of preferential treatment and raises exclusivity questions the pitch is designed to avoid.
- Journalists are not informed that other journalists received the same pitch. This is not concealment — it is the standard practice of multi-source offers in institutional correspondence. If a journalist asks directly, answer truthfully: "Yes, this is a multi-recipient source offer. VERDICT does not offer exclusivity."
- A record of the journalist list, send timestamp, and any subsequent responses is maintained in the media outreach tracker for audit purposes.

### Timing

Incident-driven pitches have three time windows, each with different operational implications:

- **Window A: 0–12 hours after incident disclosure** — highest coverage impact, but highest draft risk. Verify the VERDICT data is still accurate against rapidly evolving incident details (CVE descriptions are sometimes revised in the first hours). If the VERDICT evaluation is older than 30 days, verify that the affected platform's situation has not materially changed.
- **Window B: 12–48 hours after disclosure** — peak coverage period. Most journalists are drafting or expanding their coverage in this window. This is the operationally safest and highest-response window.
- **Window C: 48–72 hours after disclosure** — coverage cycle is closing. Pitches here are directed toward longer-form follow-up coverage rather than initial incident reporting. Adjust the story hook accordingly.

After 72 hours, switch to Template 1 (Cold pitch) or Template 5 (Research report pitch) depending on the retrospective angle.

### Subject line

Incident-driven subject lines must immediately signal urgency and specificity. Use one of the following patterns:

- CVE-driven: `{{INCIDENT_NAME}} coverage — VERDICT data on {{AFFECTED_PLATFORM_COUNT}} affected platforms`
- KEV listing: `{{INCIDENT_NAME}} added to CISA KEV — VERDICT evaluation data available`
- Supply chain: `{{SUPPLY_CHAIN_EVENT}} — VERDICT has pre-incident data on downstream platforms`
- Vendor event: `{{VENDOR_EVENT}} — VERDICT evaluation of {{PLATFORM_NAME}}, published {{EVALUATION_DATE}}`

Subject line rules:

- Include a specific incident identifier (CVE number, KEV catalog entry, dated advisory URL, or verbatim event name). Vague subject lines get ignored in the incident news cycle inbox.
- Character limit and anchor rules from Template 1 apply (≤ 80 chars, no questions).
- Sensationalism is permitted to a **limited degree** in subject lines for incident-driven pitches, where the urgency is factually grounded. The word "urgent" is never used, but the incident name itself carries the urgency.
- Do not use "BREAKING" or "EXCLUSIVE" in any form. The pitch is a multi-recipient source offer, and these words falsely frame it otherwise.
- Do not include a score or tier in the subject line for incident pitches. The data-point-first framing belongs in the body; the subject needs to immediately identify the incident and VERDICT's relevance.

### Opening paragraph (Incident scoop)

The incident scoop opening differs from Cold pitch openings in three ways: it leads with the incident rather than with VERDICT identification, it states the multi-recipient nature implicitly through neutral framing, and it compresses to three short sentences. Draft the opening in the following structure:

```
Dear {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

{{INCIDENT_NAME}} was disclosed on {{INCIDENT_DATE}} ({{INCIDENT_SOURCE_URL}}). VERDICT's pre-incident evaluation data on {{AFFECTED_PLATFORM_COUNT}} affected or adjacent platforms may be useful for your coverage.

A bias disclosure (abbreviated for time-sensitivity, full disclosure at https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine): VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates.
```

The incident opening is fixed in structure. The three sentences carry three functions: incident identification with source, VERDICT relevance statement, and compressed bias disclosure.

Rules for the incident scoop opening:

- Lead with the incident, not with VERDICT. The journalist is already in incident-coverage mode; establishing the incident first aligns with their mental context.
- Include the incident source URL in the first sentence. Journalists verify incident details before reading further; providing the source immediately reduces verification friction.
- The bias disclosure is compressed to one sentence because of the time-sensitivity framing. The parenthetical "(abbreviated for time-sensitivity, full disclosure at {{FRAMEWORK_URL}})" acknowledges the compression and directs the journalist to the full disclosure if they want to verify.
- Never imply that VERDICT has proprietary or insider information about the incident. The value is in pre-incident public data, not in incident investigation. Lines like "VERDICT has been tracking this" read as speculation or investigation claim — avoid.
- Do not reference the journalist's beat or prior coverage. Incident-driven pitches are topic-driven, not relationship-driven. Relationship context belongs in Template 3 (Warm pitch).

### Incident data body

The incident data body is tightly structured to support rapid journalist integration. The format prioritizes immediate verifiability and cross-platform context:

```
Pre-incident VERDICT data on affected / adjacent platforms:

| Platform | Score | Tier | Evaluation date | Relevant dimension |
|----------|-------|------|-----------------|--------------------|
| {{PLATFORM_1}} | {{SCORE_1}}/85 | {{TIER_1}} | {{DATE_1}} | {{DIMENSION_1}} |
| {{PLATFORM_2}} | {{SCORE_2}}/85 | {{TIER_2}} | {{DATE_2}} | {{DIMENSION_2}} |
| {{PLATFORM_3}} | {{SCORE_3}}/85 | {{TIER_3}} | {{DATE_3}} | {{DIMENSION_3}} |

Cross-platform observation relevant to {{INCIDENT_NAME}}:

{{CROSS_PLATFORM_OBSERVATION}}

Full evaluations (each page contains the complete dimension-level analysis and cited sources):

- {{PLATFORM_1}}: {{EVALUATION_URL_1}}
- {{PLATFORM_2}}: {{EVALUATION_URL_2}}
- {{PLATFORM_3}}: {{EVALUATION_URL_3}}

```

Rules for the incident data body:

- The tabular format is intentional. Journalists on deadline scan tables more efficiently than prose. A 3–4 row table with clear column headers delivers the core data in seconds.
- Include 2–5 platforms in the table. Fewer than 2 signals insufficient relevance; more than 5 overwhelms the journalist's integration window.
- The "Relevant dimension" column names the specific VERDICT dimension most relevant to the incident (e.g., R for supply chain, D for privacy-driven incidents, T for transparency-driven incidents). This guides the journalist to the incident-relevant angle without editorializing.
- The cross-platform observation paragraph is 2–4 sentences. It states a factual pattern visible across the table rows, not an interpretation. Example: "Three of four affected platforms were evaluated before 2026-03 and have not been re-evaluated since; their R-dimension scores do not reflect post-incident responses." Not: "This incident reveals systemic problems in the ecosystem."
- Every platform listed in the table must be a platform VERDICT has actually evaluated and published. Never include platforms as "adjacent" if they are not in the published dataset — this is a hallucination failure mode that destroys institutional credibility.
- The framework URL appears once, at the end, as a single line. The table and observation are the substance; the framework link is verification infrastructure.

### Urgency-respecting closing

The incident scoop closing is compressed and operationally focused:

```
If VERDICT data fits your coverage angle, the evaluation pages linked above contain the complete public-source citations for independent verification. No briefing required.

For follow-up questions during active coverage: press@getverdict.fyi
For factual corrections: vendor@getverdict.fyi

VERDICT is an independent AI agent trust evaluation index, operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan). All evaluations use public data exclusively. This is a multi-recipient source offer; VERDICT does not offer exclusivity.

— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)
```

Rules for the Template 4 closing:

- "No briefing required" is the operative sentence. It removes the implicit pressure to engage VERDICT before publishing and signals that the data is immediately usable.
- The multi-recipient disclosure ("This is a multi-recipient source offer; VERDICT does not offer exclusivity") appears in the closing, not hidden. This is the honest framing of Core principle 4 made visible in the pitch itself.
- The identity statement remains (forwarding resilience, especially important in fast-moving incident coverage where pitches may be forwarded to editors for rapid decisions).
- Two email addresses are listed with compressed descriptions ("follow-up questions during active coverage" and "factual corrections"). The adjective "during active coverage" signals Operations is available for time-sensitive questions without committing to a briefing format.
- The signature remains `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)`.

### Template 4 quality check

Before sending an incident-driven scoop offer, verify each of the following. Incident pitches have the tightest quality bar because they deploy multi-recipient simultaneously in a live news cycle; errors amplify across all recipients at once.

Incident validity checks:

- The incident was publicly disclosed within the last 72 hours
- The incident meets substantive threshold (CVSS 7.0+, CISA KEV listing, or independent multi-source confirmation)
- The incident source URL in the opening resolves to an authoritative primary source (vendor advisory, NVD, CISA, or first-party press release) — not a secondary report
- The incident details have not been materially revised since VERDICT's evaluation data was collected

Data accuracy checks:

- Every platform in the table has a published VERDICT evaluation — no adjacent-platform hallucinations
- Every score and tier in the table matches the current published evaluation, not a cached older value
- Every evaluation URL resolves to the live evaluation page
- The cross-platform observation is a factual pattern derivable from the table, not an interpretation
- The "Relevant dimension" column matches the incident's structural character (R for supply chain, D for privacy, T for transparency, etc.)

Multi-recipient coordination checks:

- The journalist list has been finalized before drafting (typical 4–8 journalists)
- The pitch text is identical across all recipients except salutation
- The send window is coordinated to fit within 30 minutes
- The journalist list, send timestamp, and pitch variant are recorded in the media outreach tracker
- No journalist on the list has previously declined VERDICT pitches with a firm "stop pitching me" signal

Format and time-sensitivity checks:

- The bias disclosure uses the compressed form with "(abbreviated for time-sensitivity, ...)" parenthetical
- The Markdown table renders cleanly in plain text if the journalist's email client does not render Markdown — verify by pasting the table into a plain text editor
- Total pitch length is under 400 words (incident pitches are shorter than Cold pitches)
- No superlative language, no "BREAKING," no "EXCLUSIVE"
- Subject line is ≤ 80 characters and includes a specific incident identifier

### Markdown rendering consideration

Most journalists' email clients render Markdown inconsistently. The Template 4 incident data body uses a Markdown table, which presents a rendering risk. Operations must address this risk before sending:

**Option A — Markdown-native delivery (preferred when confirmed):**

If the journalist is known to use a Markdown-rendering client (Superhuman, HEY, Gmail with certain extensions, or any text-first interface), send the Markdown as-is. The table renders and the pitch achieves maximum visual clarity.

**Option B — Plain text equivalent with structured spacing:**

If the journalist's email client is unknown or uses traditional rendering (Outlook, older Apple Mail configurations, web-based Gmail without extensions), convert the table to a plain text equivalent:

```
Pre-incident VERDICT data on affected / adjacent platforms:

  Platform          Score    Tier    Eval date     Relevant dim
  ----------------  -------  ------  ------------  -------------
  aider             52/85    B       2026-04-21    R
  cline             48/85    C       2026-03-15    R
  continue          55/85    B       2026-02-20    R

(each listed platform has a full evaluation page linked below)
```

- Align columns with consistent spacing (use monospace mental model even in plain text; journalists recognize this pattern)
- Use hyphen underlines (`---`) for header separation
- Keep column widths reasonable to fit in a standard 80-character mail width
- Do not use pipe characters (`|`) in plain text mode — they render as literal pipes and create visual clutter

**Option C — HTML table (emergency fallback only):**

If both Markdown and plain text are insufficient and the journalist requires rich formatting (e.g., specific editor requirements), an HTML table can be embedded. This is operationally rare and requires internal approval because HTML emails from `press@getverdict.fyi` have deliverability considerations (spam filtering sensitivity).

Default to Option A or Option B based on journalist research. Option C is only used with explicit Strategy guidance.

### Post-dispatch monitoring

Incident-driven pitches require tighter post-dispatch monitoring than Cold or Warm pitches, because the news cycle moves in hours.

Within 2 hours of dispatch:

- Verify send success for all journalists on the list
- Monitor for immediate replies (auto-replies classified as no-action; substantive replies routed per Template 6 process)
- Check incident source for material updates; if the incident details have shifted in a way that affects the pitch accuracy, prepare a correction advisory

Within 24 hours of dispatch:

- Scan each journalist's publication for any coverage referencing VERDICT or the pitched data
- Record coverage in the media outreach tracker (journalist, publication, publication URL, citation format used, whether framework URL was included in citation)
- If a journalist publishes without citing VERDICT but uses data that clearly originated from the pitch, do not intervene — this is a standard outcome and does not warrant correction

Within 72 hours of dispatch:

- Close the incident pitch cycle in the tracker
- Generate a summary log entry for Discord `#media-outreach` channel (per `discord_logging_rules.md` format)
- If specific journalists engaged substantively, mark them as warm contacts for future Template 3 pitches
- If no journalist engaged, this is a normal outcome — not all incident pitches generate coverage, and this is not a signal to adjust the template

Escalation situations during post-dispatch are handled per the internal escalation procedure. One non-escalation case is handled directly:

- Any journalist publishes coverage that misrepresents VERDICT data → draft a polite factual correction under Template 6 process, routed through Strategy for approval before sending

## Template 5: Research report pitch

Use this template when VERDICT publishes a substantive research report — typically a quarterly analysis, a category-wide synthesis, a framework update, or a cross-platform longitudinal study — and the report is ready for journalist outreach. The research report pitch positions VERDICT as the source of original independent analysis, not as a source offering evaluation data from the live dataset.

Research reports are structurally different from incident pitches and individual evaluations. They are **pre-planned, pre-drafted, and pre-QA'd artifacts** with their own publication process. The pitch that accompanies them is secondary to the report itself; the report must stand on its own merit. This template enforces that the pitch respects the report's primacy.

### When this template applies

All four conditions must be met:

- A VERDICT research report has been published on getverdict.fyi and is accessible via a stable URL
- The report has passed QA Protocol v1.1 FULL mode in its entirety
- The report's findings are novel relative to individual evaluation pitches (not a repackaging of previously pitched data)
- At least 72 hours have passed since any related incident pitch (Template 4) to avoid dual-pitch fatigue with the same journalist list

Do not use this template for:

- Individual platform evaluations (use Template 1, 2, or 3 depending on journalist relationship)
- Incident-driven findings that should be pitched under time pressure (use Template 4)
- Internal methodology memos or draft working papers (these are not ready for external pitch)
- Reports that have not been published publicly (no pre-publication pitches, Core principle 6)

### Research report categories

VERDICT research reports fall into four categories, each with specific pitch considerations:

**Category A: Quarterly analysis**

- Published once per calendar quarter
- Covers the dataset state as of quarter end (e.g., Q1 2026 report covers data through 2026-03-31)
- Typical journalist targets: industry analysts, AI industry journalists at major publications (The Register, TechCrunch, Wired, Ars Technica), enterprise security journalists
- Pitch window: within 2 weeks of publication, before data becomes stale

**Category B: Category-wide synthesis**

- Covers all platforms within a defined category (e.g., "AI Coding Agents Q1 2026," "Browser Agents Q1 2026")
- Published as category expansions warrant, not on fixed schedule
- Typical journalist targets: journalists who cover the specific category
- Pitch window: within 1 month of publication

**Category C: Framework update**

- Covers changes to the VERDICT framework itself (v0.4, v0.5, etc.)
- Published when framework version is updated (signaled in the engine repository)
- Typical journalist targets: methodology-focused journalists, research methodology publications, open-source analysis channels
- Pitch window: immediately after publication, with longer engagement window (1–2 months)

**Category D: Cross-platform longitudinal study**

- Covers a specific dimension or pattern across multiple platforms over time (e.g., "R-dimension evolution across 50 platforms, 2024–2026")
- Published when sufficient longitudinal data accumulates
- Typical journalist targets: investigative journalists, policy-focused publications
- Pitch window: within 1 month of publication

### Timing

Research report pitches are not time-sensitive in the incident sense, but they have their own timing considerations:

- Send the pitch 24–72 hours after the report is published on getverdict.fyi. This gives the report time to be indexed by search engines and allows the Operations Project to confirm stable URL accessibility.
- Pitches sent more than 2 weeks after publication for Category A (Quarterly) lose currency. Journalists prefer fresh findings.
- Framework update pitches (Category C) have a longer window because methodology updates are inherently less time-sensitive.
- Do not pitch research reports during a major industry news cycle that would bury the coverage (e.g., a major acquisition announcement, a large-scale incident). Delay by 3–7 days.
- Avoid pitching research reports on Fridays or major public holidays in the target publication's jurisdiction. Reports are substantive reading; journalists engage with them during the work week.

### Subject line

Research report pitches prioritize specificity over urgency. Use one of the following patterns:

- Quarterly: `VERDICT {{REPORT_QUARTER}} report — {{PLATFORMS_COVERED}} platforms, {{KEY_FINDING}}`
- Category: `VERDICT {{CATEGORY_NAME}} {{REPORT_QUARTER}} report — {{KEY_FINDING}}`
- Framework: `VERDICT framework {{FRAMEWORK_VERSION}} — {{KEY_CHANGE}}`
- Longitudinal: `VERDICT longitudinal study — {{STUDY_SUBJECT}}, {{TIME_RANGE}}`

Subject line rules:

- Include the report category signal (quarterly / framework / longitudinal) for immediate journalist orientation. This helps journalists triage against their own editorial calendar.
- A key finding or key change appears in the subject line. If the finding cannot be compressed to a subject-line fragment, the pitch structure is weak and should be restructured.
- Character limit and anchor rules from Template 1 apply (≤ 80 chars, no sensationalism, no questions).
- Do not use "NEW" or "LATEST" in subject lines — the date context in the body makes these redundant, and journalists interpret "NEW" as marketing language.
- Do not use "EMBARGOED" unless a formal embargo is in place, which is operationally unusual for VERDICT and requires internal approval.

### Opening paragraph (Research report)

The research report opening differs from other templates in that it leads with the report as the primary artifact, positions VERDICT as the research publisher, and offers the journalist direct access to the full report rather than compressed data points. Draft the opening in the following structure:

```
Dear {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

VERDICT has published {{REPORT_TITLE}} ({{REPORT_URL}}), a {{REPORT_CATEGORY_DESCRIPTOR}} covering {{REPORT_SCOPE_SUMMARY}}. I noticed your {{PUBLICATION_SECTION}} coverage of {{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE_TITLE}} ({{RECENT_RELEVANT_COVERAGE_URL}}), and thought this report may inform a story on {{ADJACENT_TOPIC}}.

A bias disclosure note: VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates. This disclosure is included in the report and in all VERDICT communications.
```

The research report opening is fixed in structure. The three sentences carry exactly three functions: report announcement with direct URL, journalist relevance signal through prior coverage, and bias disclosure.

Rules for the research report opening:

- Lead with the report title and URL. Unlike other pitch templates, the research report exists as a standalone artifact; the journalist should be able to access it immediately without reading the pitch body.
- The `{{REPORT_CATEGORY_DESCRIPTOR}}` phrase varies by category: `quarterly analysis`, `category-wide synthesis of {{CATEGORY_NAME}}`, `framework update documenting the move to {{FRAMEWORK_VERSION}}`, or `longitudinal study of {{STUDY_SUBJECT}}`. Use the phrase that accurately describes the report.
- The journalist relevance signal uses the same structural logic as Template 1: cite a specific prior article by the journalist. Without this signal, the pitch reads as a blast.
- The bias disclosure uses the Cold pitch form (with "This disclosure is included in the report and in all VERDICT communications"), not the abbreviated Template 4 form. Research reports have standard timing, not incident urgency, and full disclosure appropriate to that timing.
- Do not describe the report with superlatives ("groundbreaking," "unprecedented," "definitive"). The report's findings speak through their data, not through adjectives.

### Report summary body

The report summary body provides a structured overview that allows journalists to assess the report's relevance without reading it in full. This is structurally different from Template 1's story hook because research reports contain multiple findings, not a single hook:

```

- Dataset size: {{PLATFORMS_COVERED}} platforms evaluated for this report
- Time period: {{REPORT_TIME_PERIOD}}

   - Supporting data: {{FINDING_1_DATA_POINT}}
   - Where in the report: {{FINDING_1_SECTION_REFERENCE}}

2. {{FINDING_2_SUMMARY}}
   - Supporting data: {{FINDING_2_DATA_POINT}}
   - Where in the report: {{FINDING_2_SECTION_REFERENCE}}

3. {{FINDING_3_SUMMARY}}
   - Supporting data: {{FINDING_3_DATA_POINT}}
   - Where in the report: {{FINDING_3_SECTION_REFERENCE}}

The full report ({{REPORT_URL}}) contains the complete methodology note, all findings, dimension-level data, and public sources cited for each finding.
```

Rules for the research report body:

- Three findings are selected and ordered by relevance to the journalist's beat, not by the report's internal structure. A security-focused journalist sees R and T dimension findings first; a business-focused journalist sees competitive dynamics findings first.
- Each finding includes a summary (one sentence), a specific data point (number or named entity), and a section reference so the journalist can verify in the full report.
- Never present more than three findings, even if the report contains more. A journalist who reads this pitch with interest will open the full report; a journalist overwhelmed by findings will skip the pitch entirely.
- Never summarize the report's conclusions in a narrative. The pitch is a structured index into the report, not a Cliff's Notes version. If the journalist wants the narrative, they read the report.
- Section references use the report's own heading convention (e.g., `Section 3.2: Dimension-level shifts`). This supports rapid verification and signals that the Operations Project understands the report's internal structure.
- The final sentence directs the journalist to the full report as the authoritative source. This reinforces that the pitch is a signpost, not a replacement.

### Framework integration reference

After the report summary, include a framework integration reference that clarifies the report's methodological basis:

```
Methodological basis:

- Framework version used: VERDICT v0.3.1
- Framework documentation: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine
- Scoring rubric: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine/blob/main/ENGINE.md
- Known fact corrections applied: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine/blob/main/KNOWN_FACTS.md

The report applies the published framework without modification. Findings derive from public-source data; no proprietary or vendor-provided data is used in VERDICT research.
```

Rules for the framework integration reference:

- Always cite the specific framework version used. Reports published during a framework transition (e.g., v0.3.1 → v0.4) must clearly state which version applies to which platform.
- The four URLs (framework root, ENGINE.md, KNOWN_FACTS.md, and the report itself) give the journalist complete methodological verification paths. Do not omit any of these.
- The closing sentence ("no proprietary or vendor-provided data is used") is factually important. Journalists covering research reports often check for hidden data sources or vendor involvement; the explicit denial reduces that friction.
- If the report includes a methodological change or exception (rare), note it here with an inline cross-reference to the report section where the exception is documented. Never hide methodological exceptions in the body of the report; surface them in the pitch.
- Do not describe the framework as "rigorous," "comprehensive," or with any self-praising adjective. The framework's CC-BY 4.0 status and public documentation speak for themselves.

### Interview availability statement

Research reports, unlike incident pitches, permit a brief interview availability statement. This is the one template where limited availability for journalist conversation is appropriate, because research reports warrant deeper discussion than data-point offers.

The statement is placed between the report summary body and the closing. Use the following wording verbatim:

```
If your editorial angle benefits from a conversation about the report's methodology, findings, or underlying dataset, VERDICT is available for a brief written Q&A (typically 3–5 questions by email, responded to within 48 hours). Live calls are not offered at this time; the written format preserves the factual precision the report standards require.
```

Rules for the interview availability statement:

- The statement is verbatim. Do not adjust the Q&A parameters (3–5 questions, 48-hour response) because inconsistent offers across journalists create uneven expectations.
- Live calls are not offered. This matches Core principle 6 (no pre-publication coordination) in substance: written Q&A produces a record, live calls do not. Written format also allows QA Protocol v1.1 to apply to responses.
- Do not offer the Q&A unsolicited in Cold pitches. This template is the only appropriate place to offer it, because the depth of a research report justifies the conversation format.
- If a journalist accepts the Q&A offer, treat their reply as a Template 6 inquiry (Response to media inquiry process, covered in Part 17).

### Closing (Research report)

Close the research report pitch with a structure that reinforces the report's self-sufficiency and provides clear paths for journalist engagement:

```
Full report: {{REPORT_URL}}

For follow-up questions on specific findings: press@getverdict.fyi
For factual corrections with supporting documentation: vendor@getverdict.fyi

The next VERDICT research report is scheduled for {{NEXT_REPORT_DATE}} ({{NEXT_REPORT_CATEGORY}}). Future coverage building on the framework remains current through the quarterly publication cadence and any interim re-evaluation triggers.

VERDICT is an independent AI agent trust evaluation index, operated by ZinovaCreation (Japan). All evaluations use public data exclusively. No vendor funds, sponsors, or receives pre-publication access to evaluations.

— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)
```

Rules for the Template 5 closing:

- The next scheduled report reference is structural. It signals to the journalist that VERDICT is a continuous research publisher, not a one-off source, and that coverage built on the current report will remain relevant through the publication cadence.
- Two contact routings are listed. Unlike Template 4 (incident), research report pitches attract factual correction requests more often because reports contain more claims than individual evaluation pitches; the `vendor@` routing is operationally important.
- The identity statement block is repeated (forwarding resilience rationale from Template 1). Research reports are sometimes forwarded internally (to editors, fact-checkers, research assistants), and the repeated identity supports comprehension in isolation.
- Do not offer briefing calls, embargoed access, or expert commentary unless the journalist explicitly requests them. The report is the briefing; additional meetings are supplementary, not foundational.
- The signature remains `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)`.

### Template 5 quality check

Before sending a research report pitch, verify each of the following. If any item fails, revise or defer until it can be satisfied.

Report readiness checks:

- The report has been published on getverdict.fyi with a stable URL
- The report URL has been tested and resolves reliably
- The report has passed QA Protocol v1.1 FULL mode in its entirety (all three passes CLEAR)
- The report's findings are distinct from previously pitched content (not a repackaging)

Content accuracy checks:

- All three findings in the pitch are present in the full report with the claimed section references
- Data points in the pitch match the report's data exactly (no rounding discrepancies, no typos)
- Framework version cited matches the report's actual methodological basis
- Section references in the pitch use the report's actual heading conventions

Journalist fit checks:

- The journalist's recent coverage indicates fit with at least one of the three pitched findings
- The journalist has not received a VERDICT pitch in the last 14 days (for Cold pitches) or meets Template 3 warm relationship criteria
- The journalist's publication is an appropriate venue for research-report coverage (avoid breaking-news-only outlets)

Format and tone checks:

- Total pitch length: 450–650 words (research report pitches are longer than Cold pitches due to structured findings)
- Three findings exactly, no more
- No superlative language describing the report or VERDICT
- The bias disclosure appears verbatim in its full Cold-pitch form
- The framework integration reference includes all four required URLs

Timing checks:

- Publication date is within the category-specific pitch window (2 weeks for Quarterly, 1 month for Category/Longitudinal, longer for Framework)
- No major industry news cycle would bury the coverage at the planned send time
- The send day is Tuesday through Thursday, outside holidays

### Multi-recipient considerations for research reports

Research report pitches can be sent to single journalists or multiple journalists, depending on the report category and the strength of individual journalist fit. Unlike Template 4 (where multi-recipient is mandatory), Template 5 allows flexible recipient strategy:

**Single-recipient approach:**

Use when:
- The report's findings are highly specific to one journalist's beat
- The journalist has demonstrated strong prior engagement with VERDICT
- The report is in Category C (Framework) or Category D (Longitudinal), where methodological depth favors targeted outreach

Single-recipient pitches follow Template 5 structure without modification. No multi-recipient disclosure is needed.

**Multi-recipient approach:**

Use when:
- The report is in Category A (Quarterly) or Category B (Category-wide), where broad coverage is appropriate
- Multiple journalists share the relevant beat
- Time-distribution to 3–6 journalists does not dilute individual fit

Multi-recipient research report pitches require:

- Identical pitch text across recipients (salutation only varies)
- Send window within 30 minutes (same discipline as Template 4)
- Multi-recipient disclosure sentence added to the closing: `This is a multi-recipient source offer; VERDICT does not offer exclusivity on published research.`
- Journalist list and send timestamps recorded in the media outreach tracker

**Judgment guidance:**

When uncertain between single-recipient and multi-recipient approach, default to single-recipient for Categories C and D, multi-recipient for Categories A and B. If a specific journalist has been requesting research report access via Template 6, prioritize them for single-recipient first-look regardless of category.

## Template 6: Response to media inquiry

Use this template when a journalist has contacted VERDICT directly with a request — a data query, a clarification question on a published evaluation, a request for additional context on a story the journalist is developing, or a follow-up to prior outbound pitches. Template 6 is the only Channel C (inbound) template and codifies VERDICT's responsive posture.

Inbound inquiries are a significant institutional opportunity because the journalist has self-identified as interested. The response quality directly affects whether this journalist becomes a warm contact (eligible for future Template 3 pitches) or fades after a single interaction. This template enforces the discipline required to convert inquiries into durable relationships without overstepping into promotional posture.

### When this template applies

Any of the following inbound inquiry types:

**Type I: Data query**

- Journalist requests specific data from the VERDICT dataset not immediately visible on the evaluation page
- Example: "What was aider's D-dimension score in the original Q1 evaluation before any re-evaluation?"

**Type II: Clarification question**

- Journalist asks about methodology, scoring logic, or interpretation of a published finding
- Example: "How does VERDICT distinguish between R and C dimensions when evaluating sandbox design?"

**Type III: Context request**

- Journalist developing a story requests additional background VERDICT might provide on a published platform or trend
- Example: "I'm writing about AI coding agents and would find it useful to understand the common patterns in VERDICT's top-scoring and bottom-scoring platforms in this category."

**Type IV: Follow-up to prior outbound pitch**

- Journalist responds to a Template 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 pitch with questions or requests for deeper data
- Example: "Thanks for the scoop offer on the LiteLLM incident. Can you clarify which platforms in your affected list use litellm as a direct vs. transitive dependency?"

Do not use this template for:

- Requests for pre-publication access to unpublished evaluations (decline per Core principle 2; escalations handled per the internal escalation procedure)
- Requests for exclusivity on pitched data (decline per Core principle 4; see below for response language)
- Requests for VERDICT commentary on a story's framing or conclusions (provide data only, not narrative; see Rules section below)
- Factual correction claims with supporting documentation (route to `vendor@getverdict.fyi` and apply vendor notification template Template 3: Factual correction response, even though the requester is a journalist rather than a vendor)

### Response time target

Respond within:

- **24 hours** for Type IV (follow-up to prior pitch): the journalist is actively engaged, and response speed directly affects whether their story incorporates VERDICT data
- **48 hours** for Types I, II, and III (direct inquiries): these allow thoughtful response without urgency pressure
- **2 business days for substantive requests requiring research**: if the inquiry requires checking the dataset, verifying framework interpretation, or consulting published sources, acknowledge receipt within 24 hours with an estimated detailed response time

Do not respond outside business hours if avoidable. Email sent at 02:00 JST suggests VERDICT operates 24/7, which is an overreach for a small operation. Respond during JST business hours (10:00–18:00) and let the journalist's email client handle time zone display.

### Subject line

The Template 6 subject line depends on whether the reply continues a journalist-initiated thread or starts a new thread:

**Reply format (preferred):**

Reply within the journalist's original thread, keeping their subject line unchanged with `Re:` prefix added by the mail client. This is the default for all Template 6 responses.

**Standalone format (only when threading is not available):**

If the journalist's original message came through an alternative channel (e.g., LinkedIn DM referenced a contact via `press@`, or the journalist's email has a strange subject line that misrepresents the topic), start a new thread:

- `VERDICT reply on {{INQUIRY_TOPIC}} — {{JOURNALIST_NAME}} ({{PUBLICATION}})`

Subject line rules:

- Default to reply threading. Breaking threads creates duplicate correspondence in the journalist's inbox, which they dislike.
- Standalone subject lines include the journalist name and publication so Operations can route incoming replies correctly if the thread continues.
- Character limit and anchor rules from Template 1 apply (≤ 80 chars, no sensationalism, no questions).
- Never use "Final response" or "Complete answer" — inquiries often generate follow-up questions, and these subject lines close the dialogue prematurely.

### Opening paragraph

The inquiry response opening is the shortest of any template because the journalist already has context. The opening acknowledges the inquiry, confirms response scope, and moves directly to content:

```
Hello {{JOURNALIST_NAME}},

Thanks for the inquiry on {{INQUIRY_TOPIC}}. Below is the specific data/context you asked about, sourced from {{SOURCE_DESCRIPTION}}.
```

That is the entire opening. No VERDICT re-introduction (the journalist knows VERDICT). No bias disclosure restatement (it appears in the closing, compressed). No extended pleasantry.

Rules for the inquiry response opening:

- Use "Hello" rather than "Dear" for informal parity with the journalist's typical inquiry tone. Inquiries tend to be conversational; the response matches that register while maintaining institutional substance.
- The thanks sentence is one sentence, specific to the inquiry topic. Generic thanks ("thanks for reaching out") signals that Operations did not read the inquiry carefully.
- The second sentence previews what follows and identifies the source. This manages the journalist's expectation and removes ambiguity about whether VERDICT is answering the question asked or pivoting.
- Never begin with an apology ("sorry for the delay"). Even if the response is later than ideal, apologies amplify rather than absorb the delay perception. Simply respond without commentary on timing.
- Do not open with "Great question!" or similar praise of the inquiry. This reads as trained service behavior, which contradicts the institutional posture.

### Response body by inquiry type

The response body varies by inquiry type. Operations selects the appropriate body structure based on the Type (I–IV) classification.

**Type I: Data query response**

Direct data retrieval with specific source citation:

```
{{DATA_REQUESTED}}:

{{DATA_RESPONSE_CONTENT}}

Source: {{SPECIFIC_SOURCE_URL_OR_REFERENCE}}

Additional context (if relevant to how this data was derived):

{{OPTIONAL_METHODOLOGICAL_NOTE}}
```

**Type II: Clarification question response**

Methodology explanation anchored to published framework:

```
On {{METHODOLOGY_TOPIC}}:

{{METHODOLOGY_EXPLANATION}}

Relevant framework section: {{ENGINE_MD_SECTION_REFERENCE}}
Full framework: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine

Where this shows up in evaluations: {{EXAMPLE_EVALUATION_REFERENCE}}
```

**Type III: Context request response**

Cross-platform pattern provision without narrative interpretation:

```
Context relevant to your {{STORY_TOPIC}} coverage:

Pattern 1 — {{PATTERN_1_DESCRIPTION}}:
{{PATTERN_1_SUPPORTING_DATA}}

Pattern 2 — {{PATTERN_2_DESCRIPTION}}:
{{PATTERN_2_SUPPORTING_DATA}}

Pattern 3 — {{PATTERN_3_DESCRIPTION}}:
{{PATTERN_3_SUPPORTING_DATA}}

These are factual patterns observable in the published VERDICT dataset. Interpretation of what these patterns mean for your story belongs to you; VERDICT provides data, not analysis framing.

Full evaluations supporting these patterns: {{EVALUATION_URLS_LIST}}
```

**Type IV: Follow-up to prior pitch response**

Continuation building on the original pitch:

```
Following up on the original pitch regarding {{ORIGINAL_PITCH_TOPIC}}:

{{FOLLOW_UP_DATA_OR_CLARIFICATION}}

Source/basis: {{SOURCE_OR_METHODOLOGY_NOTE}}

If this connects to a specific angle you are developing, the evaluation pages contain the complete dimension-level detail: {{RELEVANT_EVALUATION_URLS}}
```

Rules for response bodies:

- Each body type has a fixed structure. Do not hybridize structures (e.g., do not combine Type II methodology with Type III patterns in one response, even if the inquiry touches both). Send two separate structured sections if needed.
- Always cite source. Every factual claim in a response body must have a URL or specific reference that the journalist can verify independently.
- Type III explicitly states the interpretation boundary: VERDICT provides data, not narrative framing. This sentence is verbatim; it is operationally critical to preserve editorial independence.
- Never include data not present in the published dataset. If the inquiry requires data VERDICT does not have, state the gap explicitly: "This specific data point is not in the current VERDICT dataset; the closest published reference is [X]."
- Never fabricate methodological explanation beyond what is documented in ENGINE.md. If the framework does not address the specific scenario the journalist asks about, state that: "The framework version v0.3.1 does not explicitly address this scenario. This gap may be revisited in framework v0.4; the current evaluation applies the closest documented criterion, which is [X]."
- Type IV responses should reference the original pitch's thread context so the journalist can navigate back to the earlier correspondence. This is a time-respect signal and an auditability signal.

### Handling decline cases

Some inquiries require declining. The decline must be direct, brief, and reference the specific VERDICT principle being applied. Do not decline with excessive apology or extensive explanation — both signal discomfort with the principle, which weakens institutional posture.

**Decline: Pre-publication access**

```
Regarding your request for pre-publication access to upcoming evaluations:

VERDICT does not provide pre-publication access to evaluations under any circumstance. This is Core principle 2 (public data only, no pre-publication vendor or media sharing). Published evaluations are available on getverdict.fyi as they become live; all journalists access them simultaneously through public channels.

The framework documentation and historical evaluation dataset remain open for your reference without any time-lag: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine
```

**Decline: Exclusivity on pitched data**

```
Regarding your request for exclusive coverage of {{DATA_TOPIC}}:

VERDICT does not offer exclusivity on any data, including the material you referenced. This is Core principle 4 (no exclusivity offers) and applies to all journalists uniformly. The data is a multi-recipient source offer; your reporting on it is welcome and unencumbered, but VERDICT does not withhold the data from other journalists.

The published evaluation remains at {{EVALUATION_URL}} and is accessible to any interested party.
```

**Decline: Commentary on story framing**

```
Regarding your request for VERDICT's view on {{STORY_FRAMING_TOPIC}}:

VERDICT provides data and cites public sources; narrative framing of the story is the journalist's editorial decision. Expressing a VERDICT view on framing would introduce commentary beyond the published methodology, which would weaken the "source offer, not coverage request" posture VERDICT maintains across all media engagement.

The factual data and methodological basis remain available for you to structure the story as you see fit: {{RELEVANT_URLS}}
```

Rules for decline responses:

- Name the specific Core principle (1 through 6) being applied. This signals that the decline is structural, not arbitrary.
- Brief — three short paragraphs maximum for any decline.
- Do not apologize for declining. Apology signals discomfort; principled declines require no apology.
- Always provide an alternative path ("the published evaluation remains at...", "the framework documentation is here..."). Declining a specific request without offering any path signals obstruction rather than principle.
- Never say "I wish I could help" or "if it were up to me" — these phrases falsely suggest that the principle is negotiable at a different level. The principles are institutional, not personal.

### Closing (Inquiry response)

Close inquiry responses with a compressed structure that invites further engagement without forcing response obligation:

```
If other questions arise as you develop the story, follow-up inquiries are welcome at press@getverdict.fyi.

VERDICT evaluations are generated using Claude (Anthropic), an active participant in the AI agent market VERDICT evaluates. Full disclosure: https://github.com/zinova-lab/verdict-engine

— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)
```

Rules for the Template 6 closing:

- The "follow-up inquiries are welcome" sentence keeps the channel open. This is important for converting a single inquiry into an ongoing warm contact.
- The bias disclosure is compressed to one sentence (similar to Template 4 incident compression), with a direct link to full disclosure. The journalist already knows VERDICT; full Cold-pitch bias disclosure would be redundant.
- No identity statement block (ZinovaCreation + public data + no vendor funding). The journalist has prior context; repeating the full identity statement reads as inattention to the conversation flow.
- No contact routing lines beyond `press@`. Factual corrections during an inquiry thread are rare; if they arise, the journalist should be redirected to `vendor@` at that point, not pre-emptively.
- The signature remains `— VERDICT (via press@getverdict.fyi)`.

### Template 6 quality check

Before sending an inquiry response, verify each of the following:

Inquiry classification checks:

- The inquiry has been correctly classified as Type I, II, III, or IV
- The response body structure matches the Type classification
- If the inquiry spans multiple Types, separate structured sections are used rather than a hybrid

Content accuracy checks:

- Every factual claim in the response body cites a URL or specific reference
- No data is fabricated or extrapolated beyond the published dataset
- Methodological explanations are anchored to ENGINE.md; gaps are explicitly flagged
- Type III responses include the verbatim "VERDICT provides data, not narrative framing" sentence

Decline-handling checks:

- If the inquiry includes a decline-eligible request, the decline references the specific Core principle (1–6)
- Decline responses include an alternative path, never end with pure refusal
- No apology language appears in decline responses

Tone checks:

- Opening uses "Hello" and no "Dear"
- No "Great question!" or similar praise of the inquiry
- No "sorry for the delay" or timing apology
- Response length is proportional to inquiry substance: data queries warrant short responses; context requests may warrant longer responses; in no case does the response exceed 500 words

Timing checks:

- Response falls within the target window (24h for Type IV, 48h for Types I/II/III, acknowledgement within 24h for substantive research requests)
- Send time falls within JST business hours (10:00–18:00) unless the Type IV urgency justifies otherwise

## Sending workflow

All media pitches follow this workflow. Deviations require explicit internal approval.

### Pre-send checklist

Before sending any pitch or inquiry response, verify:

- [ ] The appropriate template has been selected from Templates 1–6 based on the decision tree in the next section
- [ ] All placeholder tokens (`{{...}}`) have been replaced with concrete values
- [ ] No placeholder token remains in the draft (search for `{{` to confirm zero matches)
- [ ] Every factual claim cites a public source (URL, document reference, or dataset location)
- [ ] The bias disclosure appears in its template-appropriate form (full form for Templates 1/3/5, compressed form for Templates 4/6, abbreviated form for Template 2)
- [ ] The journalist's recent relevant coverage has been cited (Templates 1, 2, 3, 5) or explicitly not required (Templates 4, 6)
- [ ] Subject line follows the template-specific pattern and is ≤ 80 characters
- [ ] Recipient address is verified through an authoritative source (publication staff page, author signature, LinkedIn bio)
- [ ] Sender address is `press@getverdict.fyi` (never personal email, never `hello@` or `vendor@` for media pitches)
- [ ] Pitch length falls within the template-specific range (see each template's quality check for exact limits)
- [ ] No superlative language ("leading," "pioneering," "first," "unprecedented," "groundbreaking," "definitive")
- [ ] No pre-publication coordination offer (no draft review, no embargo unless Template 4 with Strategy approval)
- [ ] No exclusivity offer for any template

### Template selection decision tree

Use this tree to select the correct template:

```
Is this an outbound pitch initiated by VERDICT?
│
├── Yes → Is this the first contact with this journalist?
│         │
│         ├── Yes → Is a specific newly disclosed incident the trigger?
│         │         │
│         │         ├── Yes (incident within 72h) → Template 4 (Incident scoop offer, multi-recipient)
│         │         └── No → Is a VERDICT research report the basis?
│         │                   │
│         │                   ├── Yes → Template 5 (Research report pitch, single or multi-recipient)
│         │                   └── No → Template 1 (Cold pitch)
│         │
│         └── No → Has the journalist previously engaged with VERDICT?
│                   │
│                   ├── Yes (qualifying history) → Template 3 (Warm pitch, one of W-1 to W-4)
│                   └── No (prior Cold pitch, no response, 14-21 days elapsed) → Template 2 (Follow-up pitch)
│
└── No (inbound) → Template 6 (Response to media inquiry, one of Types I–IV)
```

### QA Protocol application

Every outbound pitch and inquiry response passes through QA Protocol v1.1 in FULL mode, as defined in `qa_protocol_v1-1.md`. This is non-negotiable.

Apply all three passes:

- **Pass 1 (Factual Accuracy & Internal Consistency):** verify every factual claim against the published evaluation or dataset, confirm placeholder values match the record, confirm no hallucinated sources, confirm no invented section references or platform names
- **Pass 2 (Legal Risk & Inflammatory Language):** confirm no intent attribution to vendors mentioned in the pitch, no prescriptive language, no blocklist words, no absolutist claims about VERDICT's position in the market
- **Pass 3 (Audience Perception & Strategic Alignment):** evaluate how the pitch would read if forwarded by the journalist to a competitor, a regulator, or the vendor being discussed. The pitch must hold up under adversarial re-reading from all three perspectives

The QA REVIEW block output is saved to the Operations Project session log. It is not included in the sent pitch itself.

Template-specific QA emphasis:

- Template 4 (Incident): Pass 1 requires verifying every row of the incident data body table against the live evaluation pages; Pass 3 requires checking whether any affected-platform owner could interpret the pitch as implying negligence
- Template 5 (Research report): Pass 1 requires verifying every finding summary and section reference against the actual report; Pass 2 requires checking that cross-platform comparisons do not cross into vendor intent attribution
- Template 6 (Inquiry): Pass 1 requires confirming that no fabricated data or methodology is introduced; Pass 2 requires checking decline responses reference the correct Core principle

### Send timing and pacing

Media pitch timing varies by template and context:

- **Template 1 (Cold pitch):** Tuesday–Thursday, 10:00–16:00 JST (which corresponds to reasonable morning hours in European and US time zones). Avoid Fridays, weekends, and public holidays in the journalist's jurisdiction.
- **Template 2 (Follow-up):** 14–21 days after original Cold pitch, same time-of-day discipline as Template 1. Breaking event exception permits 48h follow-up.
- **Template 3 (Warm pitch):** Same discipline as Template 1, with additional awareness of the journalist's publication schedule (if they publish weekly, pitch 2–3 days before their typical publication day).
- **Template 4 (Incident):** Within 72 hours of incident disclosure, preferably in Window B (12–48h). Multi-recipient dispatch requires all sends within a 30-minute window regardless of time zone considerations.
- **Template 5 (Research report):** 24–72 hours after report publication, Tuesday–Thursday preferred. Avoid pitching during major industry news cycles that would bury coverage.
- **Template 6 (Inquiry response):** Within target windows (24h for Type IV, 48h for Types I/II/III). Respond during JST business hours (10:00–18:00) unless Type IV urgency justifies otherwise.

Batch sending of multiple Cold pitches (Template 1) in a single session is permitted for different journalists covering different story hooks. Spread sends across business hours rather than at a single minute.

### Post-send actions

Immediately after sending any pitch or inquiry response:

1. **Confirm send success.** Check the sent folder for the outgoing message and verify delivery status where available.
2. **Archive the sent message under the journalist's folder** in the `press@getverdict.fyi` mailbox. Folder naming convention: `{{PUBLICATION}}/{{JOURNALIST_NAME}}/{{YYYY-MM}}/`.
3. **Log to Discord `#media-outreach`** using the format defined in `discord_logging_rules.md`. The log entry captures: date, journalist name, publication, template used (1–6), recipient address, subject line, and next expected action.
4. **Update the media outreach tracker** in `VERDICT_worklog_v3.md` under the media engagement section. Record: send date, template used, journalist details, pitch topic, and any observable send status.
5. **Set a follow-up reminder** if the outreach warrants monitoring:
   - 14–21 days after Template 1: note whether any response was received; if none and new angle exists, consider Template 2
   - 72 hours after Template 4: close the incident pitch cycle regardless of response
   - 2 weeks after Template 5: close the research report pitch cycle
   - No follow-up for Templates 2, 3, 6: the communication concludes on its own terms

### Response monitoring

When monitoring for journalist responses, apply these classification rules:

- **No response within the template-specific window:** expected outcome for most pitches. Media outreach has inherently low response rates; silence is not a failure signal. No action required beyond tracker entry.
- **Generic auto-reply:** classify as no-response equivalent. Log and take no further action.
- **Brief acknowledgement without substantive engagement:** log, take no further action unless the acknowledgement asks a specific question.
- **Substantive inquiry response:** route to Template 6 process (one of Types I–IV).
- **Decline with reasoning ("not for us this time" with explanation):** log as qualifying interaction for future Template 3 consideration. Reply with a brief "thank you for the consideration; the framework and dataset remain open for future reference" response — this maintains the relationship without pressure.
- **Firm decline ("please stop pitching me"):** immediately remove the journalist from all future outreach lists and update the tracker. Send a short acknowledgement: "Understood; removing from VERDICT outreach. The framework remains at [URL] for any future direct reference." No follow-up.
- **Published coverage citing VERDICT:** log in the tracker, note whether the framework URL was cited alongside the specific evaluation URL. If framework citation is missing, no action — the citation norm is an expectation, not a requirement. Repeat omissions from the same journalist may inform future pitch framing.

### Escalation

Escalation triggers and their handling follow the internal escalation
procedure (held in confidential canonical, outside the public
repositories). In escalation situations Operations does not negotiate,
make legal clarifications, or offer framing concessions; such responses
are handled per that procedure.

### Discord integration

All media engagement events log to Discord `#media-outreach` per the format defined in `discord_logging_rules.md`. Common event types and their log structures:

- **Pitch sent:** date, journalist, publication, template (1–5), subject line, expected response window
- **Response received (generic):** date, journalist, response type, classification, no-action flag
- **Response received (substantive):** date, journalist, inquiry type (I–IV), Template 6 routing, next action
- **Decline received (with reasoning):** date, journalist, decline reasoning summary, qualifying-for-future-Template-3 flag
- **Decline received (firm):** date, journalist, removal from outreach list confirmed
- **Coverage published citing VERDICT:** date, journalist, publication URL, citation format observed, framework URL included flag
- **Escalation triggered:** date, journalist, escalation trigger (category), internal routing timestamp

Log entries are English-only, exclude credentials, and do not paraphrase journalist correspondence (which remains in the `press@` mailbox under retention policy).

### Channel organization

The six templates are organized into three operational channels:

**Channel A — Print journalists (relationship lifecycle):**

| Template | Use case | Part range |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Template 1 | Cold pitch to journalist (initial contact) | Parts 2–5 |
| Template 2 | Follow-up pitch (no prior response) | Parts 6–7 |
| Template 3 | Warm pitch to returning journalist | Parts 8–10 |

**Channel B — Scoop / Research offer (special opportunities):**

| Template | Use case | Part range |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Template 4 | Incident-driven scoop offer (multi-recipient) | Parts 11–13 |
| Template 5 | Research report pitch (single or multi-recipient) | Parts 14–16 |

**Channel C — Inbound (responsive):**

| Template | Use case | Part range |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Template 6 | Response to media inquiry | Parts 17–18 |

### Analyst engagement note

This document covers journalist engagement. Industry analyst engagement (Gartner, Forrester, 451 Research, IDC analysts, independent analyst firms, and academic researchers producing public analysis) has different operational patterns and is intentionally out of scope for this template. A separate `analyst_engagement_template.md` may be developed as a future Operations Project Knowledge addition when analyst relationships become material to VERDICT's distribution posture.
